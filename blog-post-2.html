Hawaii General Excise Tax (GET) Explained

By Hale Bookkeeping Team · May 30, 2024 · 6 min read

One of the most common sources of confusion for new Hawaii business owners is the General Excise Tax (GET). Unlike the sales tax systems most people are familiar with, Hawaii's GET works differently in important ways that affect how you price products and services, how you collect money, and how you file with the state.

GET Is a Business Tax, Not a Consumer Tax

A traditional sales tax is legally imposed on the consumer and collected by the business as an agent of the state. The GET is different: it is a privilege tax on the business for the privilege of doing business in Hawaii. You — the business — are legally responsible for paying the GET on your gross receipts, regardless of whether you separately collect it from your customers. If you forget to add GET to an invoice and your client doesn't pay it, you still owe it to the state.

GET Rates

The base GET rate is 4% statewide. Honolulu County (Oahu) adds a 0.5% county surcharge, making the effective rate 4.712% when passed through to customers. Other counties do not have the surcharge. The rate on wholesale transactions (sales for resale) is lower at 0.5%. Services, retail, and most business activities are subject to the standard rate.

What Is Subject to GET?

Almost everything. Retail sales, services, rentals, commissions, contracting, and even receiving interest income can be subject to GET. There are some exemptions — wholesale sales with proper resale certificates, certain medical services, some agricultural activities — but in general, if money comes into your business, assume GET applies until you confirm it doesn't. Common mistake: service businesses like consultants and cleaners that don't realize their services are GET-taxable.

How to File GET

GET is filed using Hawaii Form G-45 (periodic return) and Form G-49 (annual reconciliation). Filing frequency depends on your annual GET liability: monthly if over $4,000/year, quarterly if $2,000–$4,000, semi-annually if under $2,000. You can file online through Hawaii Tax Online (HTO). Penalties for late filing are 5% per month up to 25%, plus interest — so consistent timely filing is critical.

The Pyramiding Effect

One unique aspect of Hawaii's GET is that it can pyramid — meaning GET applies to GET. If a contractor pays GET on subcontracted labor and includes that cost in their invoice to a client, the client's full invoice is also subject to GET. This pyramiding effect increases the effective tax burden in supply chains and is something to be aware of when structuring business arrangements.
