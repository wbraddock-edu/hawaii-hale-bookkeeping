Financial Tips for Seasonal Hawaii Businesses

By Hale Bookkeeping Team · April 22, 2024 · 6 min read

Many Hawaii businesses experience dramatic revenue swings tied to tourism seasons, holiday travel, and local events. A beachfront restaurant might do 60% of its annual revenue between May and September. A surf school can see bookings drop 40% in winter. For business owners riding these waves, financial management requires year-round discipline even when the money is not flowing evenly.

Build a Cash Reserve During Peak Season

The most important principle: save aggressively during high-revenue months to fund slow-season operations. A common target is 3–6 months of operating expenses held in a liquid savings or money market account. This reserve covers rent, utilities, payroll, and other fixed costs during low-revenue periods without requiring emergency loans or drawing on personal funds. Building this reserve requires discipline — it means not spending all peak-season profits as they come in.

Create a Realistic Annual Budget

A proper budget for a seasonal business needs to account for your actual revenue pattern. Use prior years' data to project month-by-month revenue and expenses. Identify your break-even point for slow months. Plan for major expenses — equipment, renovations, marketing campaigns — to occur during or immediately after peak season when cash flow is strongest. Having a written budget makes it much easier to make good spending decisions throughout the year.

Manage Payroll Carefully

Labor is typically the largest variable expense for Hawaii seasonal businesses. Understand which positions are essential year-round versus seasonal. Hawaii's strong employee protections mean you need to follow proper procedures for seasonal layoffs and rehires. Some businesses retain a smaller core team year-round and supplement with temporary workers during peak season. Whichever approach you use, budget payroll costs accurately for each month of the year.

Separate Business and Personal Finances

This is always important, but critically so for seasonal businesses. In high-revenue months, it can be tempting to take large personal draws — then find yourself short when slow season arrives. Pay yourself a consistent, budgeted amount from your business throughout the year rather than drawing based on current cash position. This prevents the feast-or-famine personal finance cycle that many seasonal business owners experience.
