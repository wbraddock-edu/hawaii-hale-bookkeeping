Hawaii Small Business Tax Guide 2024

By Hale Bookkeeping Team · June 12, 2024 · 6 min read

Running a business in Hawaii comes with unique tax obligations. Whether you own a restaurant in Kaimuki, a boutique in Ala Moana, or a construction company in Pearl City, understanding your tax responsibilities is essential to staying compliant and maximizing your profitability.

Federal Business Taxes

As a Hawaii business, you are subject to federal income tax on your business profits. The structure of your business determines how this is taxed: sole proprietors and single-member LLCs report on Schedule C of their personal return; partnerships and multi-member LLCs file Form 1065; S corporations file Form 1120-S; C corporations file Form 1120. Most small Hawaii businesses benefit from pass-through structures that avoid the double taxation of C corporation status.

Self-employment tax (15.3% on net self-employment income) is an often-overlooked expense for sole proprietors and partners. Proper quarterly estimated tax payments help avoid underpayment penalties. The IRS expects you to pay taxes as you earn income, not just at year end.

Hawaii State Income Tax

Hawaii has a state income tax ranging from 1.4% to 11% depending on income level — one of the higher top marginal rates in the nation. Business entities also have state tax filing requirements. S corporations and partnerships file informational returns, and shareholders/partners receive K-1s reporting their share of income. Hawaii follows federal treatment on most business tax matters but has some important differences in depreciation and other areas.

General Excise Tax (GET)

Hawaii's GET is often confused with a sales tax but is fundamentally different. The GET is a tax on business activity — not a tax on the consumer's purchase — and applies at a base rate of 4% (4.5% in Honolulu with the county surcharge) on gross receipts. This means you owe GET on your total revenues before deducting expenses. Because you can legally pass the GET to customers, most businesses add it to their invoices — but you are legally responsible for remitting it regardless of whether you collect it.

Year-End Tax Planning

The best time to reduce your tax bill is before December 31st, not on April 15th. Year-end strategies for Hawaii businesses include maximizing retirement contributions, accelerating deductible expenses into the current year, timing income to more advantageous periods, and reviewing your business structure for tax efficiency. Work with a tax professional each fall to identify opportunities before the calendar year closes.
