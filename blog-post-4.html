Bookkeeper vs. CPA: What's the Difference for Hawaii Businesses?

By Hale Bookkeeping Team · May 6, 2024 · 6 min read

When your Hawaii business needs financial help, it can be confusing to know whether you need a bookkeeper, a CPA, or both. These professionals perform different but complementary functions, and understanding the difference can save you money and ensure you're getting the right expertise for each task.

What a Bookkeeper Does

A bookkeeper handles the day-to-day recording and organization of financial transactions. Core bookkeeping tasks include recording income and expenses, reconciling bank and credit card accounts, managing accounts payable and receivable, processing payroll, generating monthly financial reports, and maintaining organized records throughout the year. Bookkeepers do not typically hold CPA licenses, but many are highly skilled accounting professionals with deep expertise in small business financials.

What a CPA Does

A Certified Public Accountant (CPA) holds a state license requiring significant college education, passing the Uniform CPA Exam, and meeting experience requirements. CPAs can perform audits, provide attest services, and represent clients before the IRS in complex matters. They typically focus on higher-level tasks like tax strategy, business structure advice, financial statement preparation for lenders, and complex tax filing. CPA hourly rates are generally significantly higher than bookkeeper rates.

Why Most Hawaii Small Businesses Need Both

The most efficient and cost-effective approach for most small Hawaii businesses is a combination: a bookkeeper handles ongoing monthly maintenance while a CPA handles annual tax filings and strategic planning. Using a CPA to do monthly bookkeeping is like hiring a surgeon to take blood pressure readings — technically capable but significantly overpriced for routine work. A well-organized set of books from a skilled bookkeeper makes the CPA's tax work faster and more accurate, ultimately reducing your total professional fees.

What to Look for in a Hawaii Bookkeeper

The most important qualification for a Hawaii bookkeeper is knowledge of Hawaii-specific requirements — the GET, TDI payroll withholding, Hawaii state tax forms, and the quirks of doing business in the islands. Experience with your industry is valuable. QuickBooks certification (QuickBooks ProAdvisor) indicates software proficiency. References from other Hawaii business owners are the most reliable indicator of real-world performance.
